Fridge Foughts

Well, about two weeks ago the latch on our fridge, a fish-clad Kelvinator, broke. Since then we've been wedging it shut with a pole and a weight on top (specifically, my climbing pad). We'd already been thinking about replacing it, but there was no urgency until the door broke.

See, the thing is we live on the top floor of a 3 story apartment. But since the ceilings are 12â€³ in height, it's more like being on the top floor of a 5 or 6 story apartment. And there is an elevator, but it opens into the neighbors apartment and the locked basement, and it's quite possibly a hundred years old. It's definitely not safe to ride in. (It's open, as in you can see the walls go by, too).

And we don't know how long we're going to stay here, but it's less than 2 years, so we'd have to pick up all the kit and kaboodle and move it, maybe damage it, etc.. Which doesn't really appeal to me.

So my thought, is get a cheap and not energy-inefficient fridge (E.g. a $400 12 cubic foot Danby (like this one from Costco, which does 420kWh/year) and plan on selling it cheap when we leave it. My other crazy thought is to go nuts for the most energy efficient - viz., a Sun Frost which uses about 200kWh/year and is supposed to be 'better' at keeping food. And it can be purchased to run on DC, which is good for alternative, off-grid, energy sources. And it costs ~$3600.

Or somewhere in the middle. Which I think is more what Joan wants. And soon!

Anyways. Sun Frost is a very interesting company, and they finally answered the question Canadians have been asking forever: How do we use the power of the Canadian winter to refridgerate our food?

Using Cold Weather to Passively Assist Refrigeration

They also have some other interesting articles like personal air conditioning and an energy efficient shower.
