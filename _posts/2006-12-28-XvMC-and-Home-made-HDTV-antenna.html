XvMC and Home-made HDTV antenna

I switched the video cards and the system looks like it is doing quite well - almost - the digital audio keeps dropping out. At this point I'm wondering if this is an IRQ problem as a couple of things which are sharing IRQs (the video card and the capture cards) are sharing irq's. Or it could be a PCI bandwidth/latency problem. Or it could be the wrong driver.

I tried rolling back to the popular driver (v. 7676) but it doesn't look compatible with a 2.6 kernel, so I went to the next recommended one. Knoppmyth R5E-whatever was released, but I don't have any CD-Rs right now. I don't know if it would help anyways (especially since I am running an SVN version).

While I'm percolating on whether to solve the entire problem by throwing money at it (a new mobo, massive cpu, etc), I decided to see what I could do about raw reception. Somewhere on avsforum or possibly instructables I saw a link to this set of instructions on how to build a DIY antenna.

I dropped by the Home Depot to pickup a FM modulator to coax converter and a couple of screws and washers. I should have picked up a saw.

Anyways, I scanned the instructions and then decided to improvise on a piece of styrofoam I had around the house instead of cardboard. Any cardboard of a suitable size was already attached to a box, and someday we'll need them to move.

The styrofoam was great, except the original design had the backing support wood screwed to the front pieces. I dug up some 4 inch deck screws we'd used on the climbing wall and went to find some aluminum foil.

I had a huge roll of vapour barrier from solar insulating the skylight and windows in the summer. A perfect substitute for aluminum foil.

When it got to the wood part Joan's brother Willy had over-estimated how much we'd need for a project to seal the skylight so I had tons of the perfect size. Except no saw. I first tried a serrated multi-tool knife blade which looked like it was going to work great, except the wood split down the middle when I tried to just cut a couple of millimeters and then force it by weight. No surprise.

Next inspiration was to use the drill to do high-density horizontal, vertical and diagonal holes until it came apart easily. Then a quick file and it was ugly^H^H^H^Hacceptable.

Everything else was pretty straight-forward. It was pretty cool how it came together. Finally, the piece-de-resistance, attach the converter, sit it down on the table, unplug the amplified antenna that was plugged into the HDHomeRun and plug it in.

I have to say, I'm impressed. Before I received CBC, which is transmitting from a couple of blocks away, and barely received the carrier for a US channel.

The next plan is to unseal the skylight and point it out there, in a southerly direction. Oh and get that HD playback working correctly!
