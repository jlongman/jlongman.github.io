Ridiculous Booing Booing

I'm not certain I understand exactly what is ridiculous about a toothbrush with replaceable bristles. It's similar to a razor with replacable blades, except that typically that model is used in loss-leader economics. On the other hand if using a replacable-bristle brush reduced landfill mass by two, wouldn't that be a good thing?

I was speaking with a McGill grad student many many moons ago at Bill and Nat's place, and she pointed out that many people can only perceive the impact of their 'tribe' - which is typically 20-30 people, but maybe I think for the 'well-connected' a hundred or so. The problem with that is that you can't conceive of marginal environmental impact above the size of the tribe. You can say that, if a tribe leaves a candy wrapper each, then my favourite park will become nasty. But a tribe just walking through it won't leave any damage.

But the truth is that a group many many many times your tribe walks through the park, doing much much more damage. Which is why you should stay on the trail and try to use raised walkways or stone paths if they're available.

So, getting back to toothbrushes, if my tribe reduces their toothbrush landfill mass by only two, who cares. But if 100,000 x 'my tribe' reduce their toothbrush landfill mass by two... Hardly ridiculous.
